Stochastic Surfel Hierarchy: Variance-Driven Adaptive Sampling for Real-Time Global Illumination

Harper Research Division

Harper Engine Technical Whitepaper HE-WP-001

Abstract

We present Stochastic Surfel Hierarchy (SSH), a novel approach to real-time global illumination that achieves 2.1× performance improvement over state-of-the-art Dynamic Diffuse Global Illumination (DDGI) while maintaining equivalent visual quality. Unlike fixed probe grids that allocate uniform computational resources across the scene, SSH dynamically spawns and manages hierarchical surfel representations based on geometric complexity and lighting variance. Our key insight is that variance in the lighting signal provides an optimal metric for adaptive ray allocation—regions with high variance benefit from additional samples while converged regions require minimal maintenance. We formulate the problem as variance-weighted importance sampling over a hierarchical acceleration structure, enabling O(log n) lookup times for surfel queries. The system achieves stable 60+ FPS global illumination at 1440p resolution on RTX 4080-class hardware with a total cost of 1.8ms per frame, representing a significant advancement in production-ready real-time GI solutions.

1. Introduction

Real-time global illumination remains one of the most challenging problems in interactive graphics. The fundamental tension between physical accuracy and computational tractability has driven decades of research into approximation techniques that trade fidelity for performance. Recent advances in ray tracing hardware have shifted the landscape, enabling limited ray budgets to be allocated strategically across the scene. However, the question of optimal ray allocation remains open.

Current production solutions, exemplified by NVIDIA's RTXGI and Dynamic Diffuse Global Illumination (DDGI), employ fixed probe grids that discretize the scene into regular spatial cells. Each probe stores irradiance encoded in spherical harmonics or octahedral maps, accumulating lighting information over multiple frames through temporal integration. While effective, this approach suffers from fundamental limitations: uniform probe density wastes resources in geometrically simple regions while providing insufficient resolution in complex areas; the fixed grid structure creates aliasing artifacts at discontinuities; and the update strategy fails to adapt to lighting dynamics.

We propose Stochastic Surfel Hierarchy (SSH), a fundamentally different approach that replaces fixed grids with an adaptive, variance-driven surfel representation. The key innovations are threefold:

Adaptive Density: Surfels are spawned and merged based on geometric complexity and lighting variance, concentrating computational resources where they provide maximum benefit.

Variance-Weighted Sampling: Ray allocation is driven by estimated variance in the irradiance signal, implementing optimal importance sampling within a fixed ray budget.

Hierarchical Acceleration: A spatial hierarchy enables O(log n) surfel queries while supporting dynamic insertion and removal as scene conditions change.

Our approach achieves 2.1× speedup over DDGI at equivalent quality, or alternatively provides substantially higher quality within the same computational budget. The system has been validated on complex production scenes including architectural interiors, outdoor environments, and dynamic lighting scenarios.

2. Related Work

2.1 Probe-Based Global Illumination

Irradiance volumes [Greger et al. 1998] introduced the concept of storing precomputed lighting in spatial data structures for real-time lookup. Light Probe Interpolation [McTaggart 2004] extended this with smooth blending between discrete samples. Modern implementations including DDGI [Majercik et al. 2019] and RTXGI combine these foundations with hardware ray tracing, achieving practical real-time performance.

Importance-Based Ray Strategies for DDGI (IS-DDGI) [Apers et al. 2023] represents the closest prior work, introducing importance sampling for ray allocation within the probe framework. Their approach achieves 1.27×-2.47× speedup over baseline DDGI. Our work differs fundamentally by abandoning the fixed grid structure entirely, enabling adaptation at the representation level rather than solely at the sampling level.

2.2 Surfel-Based Representations

Surface elements (surfels) have a long history in point-based graphics [Pfister et al. 2000]. Recent work has applied surfel representations to global illumination, including Surfel GI (GIBS) [Silvennoinen & Sloan 2022] and Lumen's surface cache [Unreal Engine 5]. These approaches cache lighting on surface-aligned elements, providing view-independent storage with natural geometric adaptation.

Our contribution extends surfel-based approaches with variance-driven adaptive density and hierarchical organization, enabling automatic resource allocation based on lighting complexity rather than relying on fixed resolution or geometric density alone.

2.3 Adaptive Sampling and Variance Reduction

Adaptive sampling techniques have been extensively studied in offline rendering [Pharr et al. 2016]. ReSTIR [Bitterli et al. 2020] demonstrated that reservoir-based resampling could dramatically improve sampling efficiency for direct lighting. Extensions to global illumination [Lin et al. 2022] have shown similar benefits for indirect lighting paths. Our variance-driven allocation builds on these foundations while targeting the specific structure of surfel-based representations.

3. Stochastic Surfel Hierarchy

3.1 Surfel Representation

Each surfel S in our hierarchy stores the following attributes:

S = {p, n, r, L, σ², M, t}

where p ∈ ℝ³ is the world-space position, n ∈ S² is the surface normal, r ∈ ℝ⁺ is the surfel radius defining its area of influence, L represents irradiance encoded as second-order spherical harmonics (SH2, 9 coefficients per color channel for 27 total), σ² is the estimated variance in the irradiance signal, M is the sample count for statistical tracking, and t is the timestamp of last update.

The total storage per surfel is 64 bytes, organized as:

struct Surfel {
    float3 position;      // 12 bytes
    float  radius;        // 4 bytes
    float3 normal;        // 12 bytes
    float  variance;      // 4 bytes
    float  sh_coeffs[27]; // 108 bytes (packed to 96 via RGB9E5)
    uint   sample_count;  // 4 bytes
    uint   timestamp;     // 4 bytes
};  // Total: 136 bytes, padded/compressed to 128

3.2 Hierarchical Organization

Surfels are organized in a spatial hierarchy using a variant of the Bounding Volume Hierarchy (BVH) optimized for dynamic updates. Each internal node stores:

struct SSHNode {
    AABB bounds;           // Axis-aligned bounding box
    uint child_indices[8]; // Up to 8 children (octree-like)
    float aggregate_variance;
    uint  surfel_count;
};

The hierarchy level L for a surfel is determined by its radius and the variance of surrounding surfels:

L(S) = max(0, ⌊log₂(coverage(S) × importance(S))⌋)

where coverage(S) is the screen-space projected area and importance(S) is derived from the surfel's variance relative to global statistics.

3.3 Variance Estimation

Accurate variance estimation is critical for effective ray allocation. We employ Welford's online algorithm for numerically stable incremental variance computation:

μₙ = μₙ₋₁ + (xₙ - μₙ₋₁) / n
Mₙ = Mₙ₋₁ + (xₙ - μₙ₋₁)(xₙ - μₙ)
σ² = Mₙ / (n - 1)

For irradiance stored in spherical harmonics, we compute variance over the dominant (DC) coefficient, which captures the majority of the signal energy. The variance σ² is updated incrementally with each new ray sample, providing a running estimate of lighting stability.

To handle temporal changes in lighting, we apply exponential decay to the sample count:

M_effective = M × exp(-λ × Δt)

where λ is the decay rate (typically 0.1-0.5 per second) and Δt is time since last update. This ensures that variance estimates remain responsive to lighting changes while maintaining stability during static conditions.

4. Variance-Driven Ray Allocation

4.1 Sampling Probability Distribution

Given a total ray budget R per frame, we must distribute rays across N active surfels to minimize overall variance in the reconstructed irradiance field. The optimal allocation follows from importance sampling theory:

P(Sᵢ) = σᵢ / Σⱼσⱼ

where P(Sᵢ) is the probability of allocating a ray to surfel Sᵢ and σᵢ is the standard deviation of that surfel's irradiance estimate. This allocation minimizes the expected squared error in the final reconstruction.

In practice, we clamp the per-surfel ray count to ensure minimum coverage:

rays(Sᵢ) = max(r_min, ⌊R × P(Sᵢ)⌋)

where r_min (typically 1) ensures that every visible surfel receives at least one ray per update cycle.

4.2 Stochastic Selection

Direct implementation of the probability distribution would require sorting all surfels by variance each frame—prohibitively expensive for large surfel counts. Instead, we employ stochastic selection using the alias method for O(1) sampling from arbitrary discrete distributions.

The alias table is rebuilt incrementally: each frame, a fraction of entries are updated based on variance changes. For N surfels, we update N/k entries per frame (typically k=16), amortizing the O(N) rebuild cost over multiple frames while remaining responsive to lighting changes.

4.3 Temporal Distribution

Ray allocation is distributed across frames using temporal blue noise to minimize perceptible variance in the accumulated result. Each surfel maintains a phase offset derived from a spatiotemporal hash:

phase(S) = frac(hash(S.id) × φ + frame × φ)
φ = (√5 - 1) / 2  // Golden ratio

Rays are allocated when the accumulated probability exceeds the phase, ensuring even distribution across frames while maintaining the variance-weighted priority.

5. Dynamic Surfel Management

5.1 Surfel Spawning

New surfels are spawned when primary ray hits occur in regions without adequate surfel coverage. The spawning criterion considers both geometric and lighting factors:

spawn_score(p) = coverage_deficit(p) × curvature(p) × lighting_complexity(p)

where coverage_deficit measures the gap between desired and actual surfel density, curvature is derived from the local surface normal variation, and lighting_complexity is estimated from the variance of nearby surfels.

Spawning is rate-limited to prevent instability: at most S_max new surfels per frame (typically 1024), prioritized by spawn_score.

5.2 Surfel Merging

Surfels are merged when their combined representation would not significantly increase reconstruction error. The merge criterion compares the variance of individual surfels against the hypothetical merged surfel:

merge_cost(S₁, S₂) = ||L(S₁) - L(S₂)|| × min(σ₁², σ₂²) × distance_factor

Pairs with merge_cost below threshold are candidates for merging. The merged surfel inherits:

p_merged = (p₁ × w₁ + p₂ × w₂) / (w₁ + w₂)  // Weighted centroid
r_merged = sqrt(r₁² + r₂²)                    // Area-preserving radius
L_merged = (L₁ × w₁ + L₂ × w₂) / (w₁ + w₂)   // Weighted irradiance
wᵢ = 1/σᵢ²                                    // Inverse variance weighting

5.3 Surfel Eviction

To maintain bounded memory usage, surfels are evicted based on a combined score of age, visibility, and contribution:

eviction_priority(S) = age(S) × (1 - visibility(S)) × (1 - contribution(S))

where age is normalized time since last visibility, visibility is the fraction of recent frames where the surfel was in the view frustum, and contribution measures the surfel's influence on screen pixels (derived from splatting coverage).

6. Implementation

6.1 GPU Pipeline

The SSH pipeline consists of five compute passes per frame:

Pass 1 - Visibility (0.2ms): Hierarchical frustum culling marks visible surfels. The BVH is traversed top-down with SIMD-optimized AABB tests.

Pass 2 - Ray Allocation (0.1ms): Variance statistics are aggregated and the alias table is partially rebuilt. Ray counts per surfel are determined.

Pass 3 - Ray Generation (0.1ms): Rays are generated for selected surfels with cosine-weighted hemisphere sampling.

Pass 4 - Ray Tracing (1.2ms): Hardware ray tracing evaluates visibility and gathers incident radiance from hit surfaces.

Pass 5 - Integration (0.2ms): Ray results are integrated into surfel SH coefficients with variance updates. Surfel spawning/merging/eviction is triggered.

6.2 Lookup and Interpolation

During shading, irradiance at a surface point is reconstructed by querying nearby surfels. We use a k-nearest-neighbor search (k=4) with weights derived from distance and normal similarity:

w(S, p, n) = exp(-||p - S.p||² / (2 × S.r²)) × max(0, n · S.n)²
L(p, n) = Σᵢ wᵢ × Lᵢ / Σᵢ wᵢ

The BVH enables O(log n) nearest-neighbor queries, with typical lookup cost under 0.05ms for 1080p resolution.

7. Results

7.1 Performance Analysis

We evaluated SSH against DDGI and IS-DDGI across a suite of test scenes on NVIDIA RTX 4080 hardware at 1440p resolution.

Performance Comparison (ms per frame at 1440p):

Bistro Exterior  | 4.2   | 3.4     | 2.1   | 2.00×
Sun Temple       | 3.5   | 2.6     | 1.6   | 2.19×

─────────────────|───────|─────────|───────|────────
Average          | 4.08  | 3.18    | 1.93  | 2.11×

7.2 Quality Analysis

Visual quality was assessed using reference path-traced images (16K samples per pixel). We computed FLIP error metrics [Andersson et al. 2020] for each method at equivalent time budgets.

At the 2ms budget, SSH achieves FLIP scores 18-34% lower (better) than DDGI, indicating superior reconstruction quality. The improvement is most pronounced in regions with complex lighting gradients and near geometric discontinuities, where adaptive surfel density provides better coverage.

7.3 Memory Usage

SSH memory consumption scales with scene complexity rather than fixed grid resolution:

8. Conclusion

We have presented Stochastic Surfel Hierarchy, a novel approach to real-time global illumination that achieves significant performance improvements over fixed-grid probe methods through variance-driven adaptive sampling and hierarchical surfel organization. The 2.1× speedup over state-of-the-art DDGI enables high-quality global illumination within the demanding frame budgets of interactive applications.

Key contributions include the formulation of variance-weighted ray allocation for surfel representations, efficient hierarchical organization supporting dynamic updates, and practical implementation strategies for GPU execution. The approach has been validated on production-scale scenes and demonstrates particular advantages for dynamic lighting scenarios and large open-world environments.

Future work includes extension to specular reflections through cone-traced surfel queries, integration with neural denoising for further ray budget reduction, and investigation of learned surfel placement strategies.
