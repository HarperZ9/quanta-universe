Temporal Irradiance Fields: Motion-Compensated Probe Caching for Dynamic Scenes

Harper Research Division

Harper Engine Technical Whitepaper HE-WP-003

Abstract

We present Temporal Irradiance Fields (TIF), extending radiance caching with per-probe motion compensation that eliminates the 2-3 frame latency of traditional temporal accumulation while maintaining stability during camera movement.

1. Introduction

Probe-based global illumination systems accumulate lighting information over multiple frames to reduce noise. This temporal integration introduces latency—lighting changes require several frames to propagate through the system. For static scenes, this latency is imperceptible. However, during camera motion or dynamic lighting, the lag creates visible artifacts: ghosting, light leaking, and temporal instability.

We introduce Temporal Irradiance Fields (TIF), augmenting each probe with motion estimation that enables predictive reprojection. Rather than blindly blending current samples with history, TIF compensates for scene motion before integration, dramatically reducing effective latency while maintaining the noise reduction benefits of temporal accumulation.

Our key insight is that probe motion can be estimated from nearby surface velocities, enabling accurate reprojection even for probes in empty space. The motion-compensated blending adapts its integration rate based on motion magnitude and confidence, providing aggressive filtering for static regions while remaining responsive to changes.

2. Motion Estimation for Probes

2.1 Velocity Field Reconstruction

Probes exist in world space, potentially distant from surfaces. Direct velocity lookup is undefined. We reconstruct probe velocity from nearby surface samples:

v_probe = Σᵢ wᵢ · v_surface(pᵢ) / Σᵢ wᵢ

where pᵢ are surface points visible from the probe (determined during ray tracing), and wᵢ are distance-weighted contributions.

For probes seeing multiple objects with different velocities (e.g., at object boundaries), we compute a weighted average that approximates the dominant motion in the probe's region of influence.

2.2 Confidence Estimation

Motion estimation confidence is derived from velocity coherence:

confidence = 1 - std(v_samples) / (mean(||v_samples||) + ε)

High confidence indicates consistent motion (rigid object or static scene). Low confidence indicates motion boundaries where aggressive temporal filtering would cause artifacts.

2.3 Reprojection

Previous frame irradiance is reprojected using the estimated velocity:

p_prev = p_current - v_probe · Δt
I_reprojected = sample(probe_history, p_prev)

Bilinear interpolation handles sub-probe offsets. Out-of-bounds reprojection triggers history invalidation.

3. Adaptive Temporal Integration

3.1 Motion-Adaptive Blend Factor

The temporal blend factor α controls the integration rate:

α = α_base · (1 + ||v_probe|| · motion_sensitivity) · confidence

where α_base (typically 0.05-0.1) determines integration for static scenes, and the motion term increases responsiveness during movement.

The update equation becomes:

I_t = α · I_current + (1-α) · I_reprojected

3.2 Variance-Guided Clamping

To prevent ghosting from incorrectly reprojected history, we clamp the reprojected value to the range of current-frame samples:

I_clamped = clamp(I_reprojected, I_current - k·σ, I_current + k·σ)

where σ is the standard deviation of current-frame ray samples and k (typically 2-3) controls clamping aggressiveness.

3.3 Disocclusion Detection

Reprojection fails at disocclusion boundaries where previously occluded regions become visible. We detect disocclusion via depth discontinuity:

disoccluded = |depth_current - depth_reprojected| > threshold

For disoccluded probes, history is invalidated and α is set to 1.0 (current frame only).

4. Implementation

4.1 Per-Probe Data Structure

struct TIF_Probe {
    float3 position;
    float3 velocity;
    float  velocity_confidence;
    SH2    irradiance_current;
    SH2    irradiance_history;
    float  variance;
    uint   valid_history_frames;
};

4.2 Pipeline Integration

The TIF update integrates into the probe update pipeline:

1. Ray trace from probes, collecting surface velocities
2. Compute probe velocity via weighted averaging
3. Estimate velocity confidence from coherence
4. Reproject history using computed velocity
5. Clamp reprojected history to current sample range
6. Blend with motion-adaptive α
7. Update variance statistics

4.3 Performance

TIF adds approximately 0.15ms to probe update cost for velocity computation and reprojection. This is offset by faster convergence requiring fewer rays per probe, yielding net performance improvement for dynamic scenes.

5. Results

5.1 Latency Reduction

We measure effective latency as frames required for 90% lighting change propagation:

Scenario              | Standard DDGI | TIF
----------------------|---------------|-----
Static Camera         | 8-12 frames   | 8-12 frames
Slow Camera Motion    | 12-18 frames  | 4-6 frames
Fast Camera Motion    | 20-30 frames  | 6-8 frames
Dynamic Light         | 15-20 frames  | 5-7 frames

TIF reduces effective latency by 2-4× during motion while maintaining identical quality for static scenes.

5.2 Temporal Stability

We measure temporal instability as frame-to-frame luminance variance:

Scenario              | Standard DDGI | TIF
----------------------|---------------|-----
Static Scene          | 0.012         | 0.011
Camera Motion         | 0.089         | 0.034
Object Motion         | 0.145         | 0.052

TIF reduces temporal variance by 2.5-3× during motion, eliminating perceptible flicker.

6. Conclusion

Temporal Irradiance Fields enable probe-based GI systems to maintain temporal stability during dynamic scenarios without sacrificing responsiveness. The motion-compensated reprojection framework is compatible with existing probe architectures and adds minimal computational overhead.

Future directions include learned motion prediction for anticipating lighting changes, integration with neural denoising for further latency reduction, and extension to specular probes with view-dependent motion compensation.
